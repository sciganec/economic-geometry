## 1. Introduction to Symmetries in Economic Geometry

In Economic Geometry, **symmetries** are transformations of the state space Ω that preserve certain structures. Just as physical laws are invariant under rotations or translations, economic relationships may be invariant under certain transformations of business configurations.

**Definition 1.1 (Symmetry).** A symmetry is a bijective mapping S: Ω → Ω that preserves some specified structure of interest.

---

## 2. The General Transformation Group

### 2.1. Definition

**Definition 2.1 (Transformation Group).** The set G of all bijective transformations T: Ω → Ω that act independently on each dimension forms a group under composition.

**Theorem 2.1 (Group Structure).** (G, ∘) satisfies the group axioms:
1. **Closure:** Composition of two transformations is a transformation
2. **Associativity:** (T₁ ∘ T₂) ∘ T₃ = T₁ ∘ (T₂ ∘ T₃)
3. **Identity:** Id(ω) = ω is the identity element
4. **Inverse:** Every bijection has an inverse

### 2.2. Generators

The group G is generated by three types of basic transformations:

1. **Agency permutations** — rearranging the four agency types
2. **Vector permutations** — rearranging the four vector types
3. **Phase permutations** — rearranging the four phase types

Additionally, **inversion** (bitwise NOT) is a fundamental symmetry.

## 3. Permutation Symmetries

### 3.1. Within-Dimension Permutations

For each dimension D ∈ {A, V, T}, we can permute the four elements.

**Example 3.1 (Agency Cycle).** A 4-cycle permuting agencies:

```
σ_A: ME → WE → THEY → YOU → ME
```

In binary codes:
- ME (10) → WE (11)
- WE (11) → THEY (00)
- THEY (00) → YOU (01)
- YOU (01) → ME (10)

**Example 3.2 (Vector Swap).** A transposition swapping EAST and SOUTH:

```
τ_V: EAST ↔ SOUTH, WEST ↔ NORTH
```

In binary codes:
- EAST (10) ↔ SOUTH (11)
- WEST (01) ↔ NORTH (00)

### 3.2. Cross-Dimension Permutations

More interesting are permutations that swap entire dimensions.

**Definition 3.1 (Dimension Permutation).** A permutation π ∈ S₃ of the set {A, V, T} induces a transformation:

```
π(a, v, t) = (x_{π(1)}, x_{π(2)}, x_{π(3)})
```

where (x₁, x₂, x₃) = (a, v, t).

**Example 3.3 (Cyclic Permutation).** π = (A → V, V → T, T → A):

```
π(ω) = (v, t, a)
```

This transforms (ME, EAST, SPRING) into (EAST, SPRING, ME) — but note that EAST is not a valid agency! This reveals an important constraint.

### 3.3. The Isomorphism Constraint

Since A, V, and T are different types of sets (agencies, vectors, phases), swapping dimensions directly is not generally meaningful. However, because all three sets are isomorphic as sets (each has 4 elements labeled with the same binary codes), we can define **dimension permutations** only after applying the isomorphism Φ.

**Definition 3.2 (Coded Dimension Permutation).** Using the binary encoding Φ: Ω → {0,1}⁶, we can permute the three 2-bit blocks:

```
π_code(b₁b₂ b₃b₄ b₅b₆) = (b_{π(1)}b_{π(1)+1}, b_{π(2)}b_{π(2)+1}, b_{π(3)}b_{π(3)+1})
```

where the blocks are indexed as block₁ = bits 1-2, block₂ = bits 3-4, block₃ = bits 5-6.

**Example 3.4.** ω = (ME, EAST, SPRING) = 10 10 10
Under π = (1→2, 2→3, 3→1):
- Original: [10] [10] [10]
- Permuted: [10] [10] [10] — actually unchanged! (all blocks equal)

A more interesting example:
ω = (WE, EAST, SUMMER) = 11 10 11
Under π = (1→2, 2→3, 3→1):
- Original: [11] [10] [11]
- Permuted: [10] [11] [11] = (EAST, SUMMER, WE) which decodes to? EAST is not an agency — so this transformation takes us out of Ω unless we reinterpret the meaning of blocks.

This reveals that **pure dimension permutations are not symmetries of the interpreted space** — they are symmetries only of the uninterpreted binary cube.

## 4. Inversion Symmetry

### 4.1. Definition

**Definition 4.1 (Inversion).** The inversion operator I: Ω → Ω is defined as:

```
I(ω) = Φ⁻¹(bitwise_NOT(Φ(ω)))
```

where bitwise_NOT flips each bit (0↔1).

### 4.2. Effect on States

**Example 4.1.** ω = (ME, EAST, SPRING) = 10 10 10
NOT = 01 01 01 = (YOU, WEST, AUTUMN)

**Example 4.2.** ω = (WE, SOUTH, SUMMER) = 11 11 11
NOT = 00 00 00 = (THEY, NORTH, WINTER)

**Example 4.3.** ω = (THEY, NORTH, WINTER) = 00 00 00
NOT = 11 11 11 = (WE, SOUTH, SUMMER)

### 4.3. Properties of Inversion

**Theorem 4.1 (Involution).** I is an involution: I(I(ω)) = ω for all ω.

*Proof.* Applying bitwise NOT twice returns the original bits.

**Theorem 4.2 (Fixed Points).** No state is fixed under inversion, because inverting all bits always changes the state (there is no binary string equal to its own complement in 6 bits).

**Theorem 4.3 (Distance Preservation).** I is an isometry: d(I(ω₁), I(ω₂)) = d(ω₁, ω₂).

*Proof.* Bitwise NOT preserves differences: if bits differ in original, they differ in complement.

**Theorem 4.4 (CES Transformation).** CES(I(ω)) = 100 - CES(ω) (approximately, given symmetric calibration).

*Proof sketch.* If the scoring functions are symmetric with respect to inversion, then CES inverts.

### 4.4. Business Interpretation of Inversion

Inversion sends a business configuration to its **structural opposite**:

| Original | Inverted | Business Meaning |
|----------|----------|------------------|
| (ME, EAST, SPRING) | (YOU, WEST, AUTUMN) | Individual creator ↔ client in harvest phase |
| (WE, SOUTH, SUMMER) | (THEY, NORTH, WINTER) | Growth-stage team ↔ declining system |
| (THEY, NORTH, WINTER) | (WE, SOUTH, SUMMER) | Declining system ↔ growth-stage team |

This duality reveals complementary business strategies.

## 5. Duality Symmetries

### 5.1. Within-Dimension Duality

For each dimension, there is a natural duality pairing:

| Dimension | Element | Dual Element |
|-----------|---------|--------------|
| **A** | ME (10) | YOU (01) |
| **A** | WE (11) | THEY (00) |
| **V** | EAST (10) | WEST (01) |
| **V** | SOUTH (11) | NORTH (00) |
| **T** | SPRING (10) | AUTUMN (01) |
| **T** | SUMMER (11) | WINTER (00) |

**Definition 5.1 (Agency Duality).** D_A: A → A maps each agency to its dual:
- D_A(ME) = YOU
- D_A(WE) = THEY
- D_A(YOU) = ME
- D_A(THEY) = WE

**Definition 5.2 (Vector Duality).** D_V: V → V:
- D_V(EAST) = WEST
- D_V(SOUTH) = NORTH
- D_V(WEST) = EAST
- D_V(NORTH) = SOUTH

**Definition 5.3 (Phase Duality).** D_T: T → T:
- D_T(SPRING) = AUTUMN
- D_T(SUMMER) = WINTER
- D_T(AUTUMN) = SPRING
- D_T(WINTER) = SUMMER

### 5.2. Combined Duality

**Definition 5.4 (Full Duality).** The full duality operator D: Ω → Ω applies duality to all three dimensions:

```
D(ω) = (D_A(a), D_V(v), D_T(t))
```

**Example 5.1.** ω = (ME, EAST, SPRING)
D(ω) = (YOU, WEST, AUTUMN) — note this is the same as I(ω)!

**Example 5.2.** ω = (WE, SOUTH, SUMMER)
D(ω) = (THEY, NORTH, WINTER) — again same as I(ω)!

**Theorem 5.1 (Duality = Inversion).** For the canonical encoding, the full duality operator D is identical to the inversion operator I.

*Proof.* Check the mapping:
- ME(10) ↔ YOU(01) — inverts bits
- WE(11) ↔ THEY(00) — inverts bits
- EAST(10) ↔ WEST(01) — inverts bits
- SOUTH(11) ↔ NORTH(00) — inverts bits
- SPRING(10) ↔ AUTUMN(01) — inverts bits
- SUMMER(11) ↔ WINTER(00) — inverts bits

Thus D acts as bitwise NOT on each 2-bit pair, which is exactly I.

## 6. Conservation Laws (Invariants)

### 6.1. Invariants Under Inversion

**Theorem 6.1 (Hamming Weight Parity).** The parity of the Hamming weight (even/odd) is invariant under inversion for states with even dimension? Let's check:

For 6 bits, |I(ω)| = 6 - |ω|. So parity:
- If |ω| is even, 6 - even = even (since 6 is even)
- If |ω| is odd, 6 - odd = odd

So **parity is invariant** under inversion.

**Theorem 6.2 (Structural Friction Relation).** For symmetric friction functions, SF(I(ω)) ≈ SF(ω). Friction is approximately invariant under inversion.

### 6.2. Invariants Under Permutations

**Theorem 6.3 (CES Invariance).** CES is invariant under dimension permutations that respect the isomorphism? Not exactly — CES depends on specific interpretations of each dimension.

**Theorem 6.4 (Distance Invariance).** All isometries (including permutations and inversion) preserve Hamming distance.

### 6.3. Invariants Under Specific Transformations

**Table 6.1. Invariants Summary**

| Transformation | Invariant Quantity | Reason |
|----------------|-------------------|--------|
| Inversion I | Hamming weight parity | 6 - |ω| preserves parity |
| Inversion I | SF (approx) | Symmetric friction function |
| Any isometry | Hamming distance | By definition of isometry |
| Agency permutations | Vector and phase structure | Only agency changes |
| Phase reversal | Seasonal patterns | T → opposite T preserves cycles |

## 7. The Complete Symmetry Group

### 7.1. Group Structure

The full symmetry group of Ω (as an uninterpreted set) is the wreath product:

```
G ≅ (S₄ ≀ S₃) × C₂
```

where:
- S₄ permutes elements within each dimension
- S₃ permutes the three dimensions
- C₂ is inversion

This gives |G| = (4!)³ × 3! × 2 = (24)³ × 6 × 2 = 13824 × 12 = 165,888 symmetries.

### 7.2. Physically Meaningful Subgroups

Not all 165,888 symmetries are economically meaningful. The relevant subgroups are:

| Subgroup | Size | Description |
|----------|------|-------------|
| **Agency permutations** | 24 | Renaming of agent types |
| **Vector permutations** | 24 | Renaming of vector types |
| **Phase permutations** | 24 | Renaming of phase types |
| **Inversion** | 2 | Structural opposition |
| **Dimensional permutations** | 6 | Swapping WHO, WHERE, WHEN (with reinterpretation) |
| **Total meaningful** | 24×24×24×2 = 27,648 | Combinations of independent renaming + inversion |

### 7.3. Practical Implications

The existence of these symmetries means that:
1. **Naming conventions are arbitrary** — the structure matters, not the labels
2. **Dual strategies exist** — for every configuration, there is an opposite configuration
3. **Classification is possible** — states can be grouped into equivalence classes under symmetries

## 8. Application: State Classification Under Symmetries

### 8.1. Equivalence Classes

Under the full symmetry group, states group into **orbits**. The size of an orbit depends on the state's symmetries.

**Example 8.1.** The state (ME, EAST, SPRING) = 10 10 10:
- Under agency permutations: 4 possibilities
- Under vector permutations: 4 possibilities
- Under phase permutations: 4 possibilities
- Under inversion: 1 opposite
Total orbit size = 4×4×4×2 / (symmetry factors) = 128 / (symmetry factors)

But many combinations yield the same state due to all dimensions having the same code.

### 8.2. Canonical Representatives

For practical analysis, we can choose canonical representatives:

| Class | Representative | Description |
|-------|---------------|-------------|
| **All 10s** | (ME, EAST, SPRING) | Beginning states |
| **All 11s** | (WE, SOUTH, SUMMER) | Peak states |
| **All 01s** | (YOU, WEST, AUTUMN) | Maturity states |
| **All 00s** | (THEY, NORTH, WINTER) | End states |
| **Mixed** | Various | Most business configurations |

## 9. Summary

| Symmetry | Operator | Effect | Invariants |
|----------|----------|--------|------------|
| **Inversion** | I(ω) = ¬ω | Structural opposite | Parity, SF (approx) |
| **Agency permutation** | σ_A | Rename agents | Vector, phase structure |
| **Vector permutation** | σ_V | Rename vectors | Agency, phase structure |
| **Phase permutation** | σ_T | Rename phases | Agency, vector structure |
| **Dimension permutation** | π | Swap WHO, WHERE, WHEN | Requires reinterpretation |

**Key Insight:** The symmetry group of Ω reveals the deep structural regularities of economic configurations. Inversion duality shows that every business model has a natural opposite, while permutation symmetries show that the naming of categories is conventional — the structure lies in the relationships, not the labels.
