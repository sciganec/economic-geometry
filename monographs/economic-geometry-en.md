### Chapter 1. Introduction: The Crisis of Economic Imagination and the Necessity of a Geometric Approach

#### 1.1. Why Traditional Linear Models Fail in Conditions of Turbulence

Twentieth-century economics formed under the influence of Newtonian physics: equilibrium, linear dependencies, predictable trajectories. From supply and demand models to production functions — everywhere the metaphor of mechanism dominated. But the 21st-century world presents a fundamentally different picture: exponential technologies, network effects, market bifurcations, sudden disappearance of entire industries.

The key problem of linear models is their inability to work with **qualitative changes**. They can predict how price will change when supply increases by 10%, but are powerless to explain why Kodak disappeared and Instagram appeared. Between these two phenomena lies not a quantitative, but a **structural gap**.

Modern economics needs a language capable of describing not only "more-less" but also "other." Such a language could be geometry — but not Euclidean geometry with its continuous spaces, but **discrete geometry of states**.

#### 1.2. From Phenomenology to Ontology: Business as a State, Not a Process

The traditional approach views business as a **process** — a sequence of actions leading from idea to profit. This view generates countless "roadmaps," "business model canvases," and "lean startup cycles." But process is only a projection of a deeper reality.

We propose a radical shift in perspective: **business is a state**. Not "what is happening," but "what exists." Not "how to move," but "where to be." This approach changes the very formulation of questions:

| Process Approach | State Approach |
|-----------------|----------------|
| How to scale? | Where is my current configuration? |
| What growth strategy? | Which operator will move me to another state? |
| What to do next? | What states are even possible? |

**Ontological turn** means we stop asking "what will happen if...?" and start asking "in what space does this occur?" Instead of predicting the future — mapping the possible.

#### 1.3. Theory Overview: SUBIT-64 as Empirical Basis of Geometry

The proposed theory does not arise in a vacuum. It is based on years of observations of the structure of successful and failed businesses, crystallized in the **SUBIT-64** model.

SUBIT is an acronym revealing three fundamental dimensions of any economic activity:
- **SU** (Subject) — Who? The agent that acts and appropriates value
- **BI** (Binding) — Where? The vector of capital accumulation
- **T** (Time) — When? The phase of structural time

The empirical discovery is that each of these dimensions has exactly **4 fundamental modalities**. Why exactly 4? We will examine this question in detail in Chapter 2, but preliminarily note: 4 is the number that ensures **structural completeness** without redundancy.

The product 4 × 4 × 4 yields 64 — a number well-known in various traditions as a symbol of completeness (64 hexagrams of the I Ching, 64 codons of the genetic code, 64 squares of a chessboard).

#### 1.4. Interdisciplinary Roots: Category Theory, Structural Psychoanalysis, Cybernetics

Economic Geometry synthesizes several powerful intellectual traditions:

#### 2.2. Structure Theorem: Ω = A × V × T as Complete Basis of Economic Reality

**Axiom 3 (Completeness).** The space of economic states Ω is the direct product of the basic spaces:

**Ω = A × V × T**

**Theorem 2.1 (On Cardinality).** The cardinality of space Ω equals |Ω| = |A| × |V| × |T| = 4 × 4 × 4 = 64.

*Proof.* Directly follows from the product rule for finite sets. Each state ω ∈ Ω is an ordered triple (a, v, t), where a ∈ A, v ∈ V, t ∈ T. The number of such triples is the product of cardinalities.

#### 2.3. The Nature of Discreteness: Why 4 and Only 4? The Argument of Structural Completeness

Why exactly 4 modalities in each dimension? The answer lies in **structural completeness** and **binary depth**.

**Argument 1 (Binary Depth).** Human thinking is fundamentally structured by binary oppositions. Two bits (4 combinations) allow representing all possible combinations of two independent binary features.

For dimension A (Agents), such features are:
- Feature 1: individual (ME, YOU) vs collective (WE, THEY)
- Feature 2: active/external (ME, WE) vs passive/internal (YOU, THEY)

Their combination yields exactly 4 types.

**Argument 2 (Semiotic Square).** The structure of 4 elements corresponds to the logical square of oppositions (Greimas), where there is affirmation, negation, affirmation of negation, and negation of affirmation.

**Argument 3 (Empirical Saturation).** Years of observation show that attempts to introduce a fifth modality lead either to duplication or to the appearance of "non-viable" configurations.

#### 2.4. Proof of Orthogonality of Dimensions (Independence of WHO, WHERE, WHEN Categories)

**Theorem 2.2 (Orthogonality of Dimensions).** Dimensions A, V, and T are independent: value in one dimension does not determine value in others.

*Proof.* To prove independence, it suffices to show that for any a ∈ A, v ∈ V, t ∈ T, an empirically possible business configuration exists.

Let us give examples for extreme cases:
- (ME, NORTH, WINTER) — individual lobbyist in the sphere of declining regulations
- (THEY, EAST, SPRING) — platform creating a new technological market
- (WE, WEST, AUTUMN) — team optimizing capital of a mature business
- (YOU, SOUTH, SUMMER) — client-oriented service in a growing market

The ability to find an example for each combination proves that dimensions vary independently.

**Corollary 2.3.** Space Ω is a complete factorial space: any combination of factor levels corresponds to some possible state.

### Chapter 4. Metric and Measures in Space Ω

#### 4.1. Hamming Distance as a Measure of Structural Differentiation of Businesses

**Definition 4.1 (Hamming Distance).** For ω₁, ω₂ ∈ Ω with binary codes b₁, b₂ ∈ {0,1}⁶, Hamming distance d(ω₁, ω₂) = ∑ᵢ₌₁⁶ |b₁ᵢ - b₂ᵢ|.

**Properties:**
1. d(ω₁, ω₂) ≥ 0, d=0 ⇔ ω₁=ω₂
2. d(ω₁, ω₂) = d(ω₂, ω₁)
3. d(ω₁, ω₃) ≤ d(ω₁, ω₂) + d(ω₂, ω₃)

**Interpretation:**
- d=0 — identical models
- d=1 — nearest neighbors (difference in one bit)
- d=2,3 — moderate difference
- d=4,5 — significant difference
- d=6 — opposite models

**Example 4.1.**
- ω_A = (ME, EAST, SPRING) = 10 10 10
- ω_B = (ME, EAST, SUMMER) = 10 10 11
- ω_C = (THEY, NORTH, WINTER) = 00 00 00

d(ω_A, ω_B) = 1, d(ω_A, ω_C) = 6, d(ω_B, ω_C) = 5

**Theorem 4.1 (Diameter of Space).** The diameter of space Ω equals 6.

#### 4.2. Geodesic Lines: Optimal Trajectories of Company Transformation

**Definition 4.2 (Geodesic).** A geodesic between ω₁ and ω₂ is a sequence γ = (γ₀, γ₁, ..., γₖ), where γ₀ = ω₁, γₖ = ω₂, d(γᵢ, γᵢ₊₁) = 1, and k = d(ω₁, ω₂).

**Theorem 4.2 (Number of Geodesics).** The number of geodesics between ω₁ and ω₂ equals (d)! / (n₀! n₁! ...), where d = d(ω₁, ω₂).

**Example 4.2.** Between ω₁ = 10 10 10 and ω₂ = 11 11 11 (d=3) there exist 3! = 6 geodesics, corresponding to different orders of changing coordinates.

#### 4.3. Structural Friction Function SF(ω): Definition and Properties

**Definition 4.3 (Structural Friction).** SF: Ω → [0,1] — measure of internal inconsistency of state coordinates.

**Axioms:**
1. SF(ω) ∈ [0,1]
2. SF(ω) = 0 for "ideal" states
3. SF(ω) = 1 for "impossible" states
4. SF increases monotonically with inconsistency

**Constructive Definition:**
SF(ω) = (α·Δ_a + β·Δ_v + γ·Δ_t + δ·Δ_pairwise) / (α+β+γ+δ)

**Example 4.3.**
- ω_good = (WE, EAST, SUMMER) = 11 10 11 → SF ≈ 0.1
- ω_bad = (ME, NORTH, WINTER) = 10 00 00 → SF ≈ 0.9

#### 4.4. Theorem on Minimum Energy: Stable States as Local Minima of SF

**Theorem 4.3 (On Minimum Energy).** Stable business configurations correspond to local minima of the structural friction function SF(ω).

**Corollary 4.4.** A business in a state with high SF tends to evolve toward decreasing SF.

**Example 4.4 (SF Landscape).**

- High SF: (YOU,NORTH,WINTER), (THEY,EAST,WINTER), (ME,NORTH,WINTER)
