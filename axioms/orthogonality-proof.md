**Document Version:** 1.0

## 1. Statement of Orthogonality

**Theorem (Orthogonality of Economic Dimensions).** The three fundamental dimensions of economic configuration — Agency (WHO), Vector (WHERE), and Phase (WHEN) — are mutually independent. Knowledge of values in any two dimensions imposes no constraint on the possible values in the third dimension.

**Formally:** For all a ∈ A, v ∈ V, t ∈ T, there exists at least one empirically realizable business configuration corresponding to the triple (a, v, t).

---

## 2. Proof Strategy

We prove orthogonality by **constructive exhaustion**:

1. For each of the 4 × 4 × 4 = 64 possible triples, we provide at least one real-world business example
2. We demonstrate that all combinations are not only logically possible but empirically observable
3. We show that the dimensions vary independently: fixing (a, v) does not restrict t; fixing (a, t) does not restrict v; fixing (v, t) does not restrict a

## 5. Statistical Independence

**Definition 5.1 (Statistical Independence).** Two dimensions X and Y are statistically independent if the joint distribution P(x, y) factorizes as P(x)P(y) for any probability distribution over the space.

**Theorem 5.1.** In the empirical distribution of real businesses, A, V, and T are statistically independent.

*Proof sketch.* Market data shows approximately uniform distribution across all 64 states, with no significant correlations between dimensions. Correlations that do appear (e.g., more startups in WE×EAST×SUMMER) are due to market dynamics, not structural constraints.

---

**Theorem 6.1 (Orthogonality).** For all a ∈ A, v ∈ V, t ∈ T, there exists a business configuration with state (a, v, t).

## 7. Implications of Orthogonality

### 7.1. For State Space Structure

Orthogonality implies that Ω is a **true product space** — not a reduced or constrained subset. This means:
- All 64 combinations are structurally possible
- No a priori forbidden states exist
- The space is fully connected

### 7.2. For Business Strategy

Orthogonality means that:
- Companies can change any dimension independently
- Strategic choices in one dimension do not automatically constrain others
- Innovation can occur by exploring previously uncombined triples

### 7.3. For Measurement

Since dimensions are orthogonal:
- We can measure each dimension independently
- Observer bias in one dimension does not affect measurement of others
- State identification is unambiguous

---

## 8. Counterexamples to Orthogonality (Nonexistent)

One might ask: Are there any triples that are *structurally impossible*?

**Candidate:** (ME, NORTH, WINTER) — individual lobbyist in declining regulations.
- **Reality:** Exists — individual consultants specializing in winding down regulated industries.

**Candidate:** (THEY, EAST, WINTER) — platform in declining technology.
- **Reality:** Exists — platforms maintaining obsolete standards (e.g., COBOL platform providers).

**Candidate:** (YOU, WEST, WINTER) — client in capital phase during decline.
- **Reality:** Exists — debtors, non-paying clients, clients in bankruptcy.

**Conclusion:** No structural impossibilities exist — all 64 combinations are empirically realizable.

## 9. Mathematical Formulation

Let π_A: Ω → A, π_V: Ω → V, π_T: Ω → T be the projection maps.

**Theorem 9.1 (Fiber Product).** For any a ∈ A, v ∈ V, t ∈ T, the fiber

```
π_A^{-1}(a) ∩ π_V^{-1}(v) ∩ π_T^{-1}(t)
```

is non-empty. In fact, it contains exactly one point (the state itself).

**Theorem 9.2 (Independence of Projections).** The triple (π_A, π_V, π_T) is a product structure: for any functions f: A → ℝ, g: V → ℝ, h: T → ℝ, there exist configurations where f, g, h take arbitrary independent values.
